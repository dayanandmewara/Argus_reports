Opening Note

CrowdStrike sits in Category B (High-Growth Global Compounder) rather than Category F: its core product is cybersecurity, not a foundation model, and it already generates GAAP-adjacent free cash flow at 30%+ margins — it graduated out of the pre-profitability Cat F bracket years ago. It earns a 🟢 AI Widener overlay because its single-agent, cloud-native architecture pools telemetry across its whole customer base into one model (Threat Graph), and the three-part data-flywheel test (proprietary data → improves with scale → attracts more users) reasonably checks out — see Tab 3 and Tab 9 for the full test. Macro relevance is mixed-to-supportive: cybersecurity spend is one of the more recession-resistant enterprise budget lines, and the mid-2026 backdrop of a resilient-but-hawkish Fed (rate hikes, not cuts, are back in the conversation) raises the discount rate used in this report without changing the demand story. What makes this stock interesting right now is a genuine growth inflection — net new ARR growth accelerated to 32% YoY in Q1 FY27 after several years of steady deceleration — landing right as CrowdStrike leans hard into securing other companies' AI agents (Falcon AIDR, Charlotte AI AgentWorks), a natural extension of its moat. What makes it challenging is that the market has already re-rated the stock enormously (roughly +130% pre-split from its March 2026 lows) into and through a 4-for-1 stock split, GAAP earnings are still barely above breakeven after outage-related charges, and the framework's own margin-of-safety discipline says this is not a price to be initiating full positions at. One adaptation to flag: because CRWD is a U.S. stock, this report uses the S&P 500 as benchmark, $ throughout, an SEC (not SEBI) disclaimer, and the "US growth" WACC band (10–12%) rather than the generic Cat B band — and because CRWD split 4-for-1 on July 2, 2026, several retail data aggregators are currently showing corrupted market-cap and P/E figures (explained in Tab 2) that this report does not use.

00.1Business Overview

CrowdStrike Holdings (Nasdaq: CRWD) sells a cloud-native cybersecurity platform — Falcon — built around a single lightweight software agent and one unified cloud data model. Founded in 2011 by George Kurtz (CEO), Dmitri Alperovitch, and Gregg Marston, the company IPO'd in June 2019 at $34/share and joined the S&P 500 in 2024. It is headquartered in Austin, Texas.

The platform now spans endpoint detection and response, cloud workload protection, identity threat protection, exposure management, Next-Gen SIEM, data protection, and — increasingly — securing enterprise AI itself (Falcon AIDR: shadow-AI discovery, AI agent behavior monitoring, prompt-injection defense). Customers land on one or two modules and expand: 50% of customers now run 6+ modules, 34% run 7+, and 24% run 8+ V, which is the mechanical engine behind CrowdStrike's 115% net dollar retention V.

The single biggest event in company history remains the July 19, 2024 global IT outage: a faulty Falcon sensor content update crashed roughly 8.5 million Windows machines worldwide, grounding flights, disrupting hospitals and banks, and triggering a wave of litigation. That litigation has mostly resolved in CrowdStrike's favor (Tab 4), except for its direct commercial dispute with Delta Air Lines, which is still open.
