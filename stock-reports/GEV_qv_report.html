Business Overview

GE Vernova LLC was spun off from General Electric on April 2, 2024, creating one of the world's largest pure-play power technology companies. With 75,000 employees and operations across the US, Europe, Asia, Middle East and Africa, GEV's technology generates approximately 25% of global electricity — comprising an installed base of ~7,000 gas turbines and ~57,000 wind turbines accumulated over 80+ years of GE's engineering heritage.

The company operates through three segments: Power (gas, nuclear, hydro, steam turbines + long-term service contracts — the profit engine), Electrification (grid solutions, transformers, switchgear, power conversion — the fastest-growing segment), and Wind (onshore and offshore turbines — currently a drag, in restructuring). The AI data center electricity boom has created an unprecedented demand surge for GEV's gas turbines and electrification products that is accelerating into a multi-year supercycle.

Economic Moat Assessment

Installed Base & Service Annuity — Gas Turbines
★★★★★
GEV has the world's largest installed fleet of gas turbines (~7,000 units). When a utility installs a GEV turbine, it is locked into GEV's Long-Term Service Agreements for 20–30 years. Turbine manufacturers develop proprietary parts, software (digital twin), and technical knowledge specific to their own machines. Third-party service is theoretically possible but practically rare — performance guarantees and liability require OEM servicing. Service backlog alone stands at $81B. This is as close to an annuity as heavy industrial gets. ROIC equivalent for services is >40%.
Gas Turbine Technology Oligopoly
★★★★☆
Only three companies in the world can manufacture large industrial gas turbines at scale: GEV (7HA series), Siemens Energy, and Mitsubishi Power. These three hold >60% of global turbine MRO revenue. New entry requires multi-decade engineering heritage, massive tooling investment, and regulatory approvals — effectively a permanent barrier. GEV leads in US market and is expanding internationally. The 7HA.03 is considered the most efficient large gas turbine in production today.
Electrification / Grid Equipment Scarcity
★★★★☆
Transformers, switchgear, and substation equipment face a structural supply constraint. Order-to-delivery times have expanded from 18 months to 3+ years for large power transformers. The Prolec GE acquisition (closed Feb 2026, $5.3B) doubled GEV's transformer manufacturing capacity in North America, giving it a meaningful advantage as competitors face multi-year lead times. Data center customers are signing up for dedicated equipment years in advance — creating sticky order commitments that competitors cannot easily displace.
Nuclear SMR Optionality (BWRX-300)
★★★☆☆
GEV's GE-Hitachi nuclear division is developing the BWRX-300 small modular reactor, with the first commercial deployment in Ontario, Canada. A Japan-US investment plan (mentioned in the Japan bilateral deal) includes GEV-Hitachi reactors. This is optionality — not yet in financial results — but positions GEV as a future beneficiary of nuclear renaissance driven by AI power demand for reliable baseload.
Wind (Onshore/Offshore) — Weak
★★☆☆☆
GEV holds the largest US onshore wind installed base, but the wind market is fiercely competitive (Vestas, Siemens Gamesa). Offshore wind economics remain challenging; GEV is embroiled in the Vineyard Wind legal dispute. The wind segment loses ~$1.5B annually at the EBITDA level. This is the primary moat weakness and investor concern — though management is actively restructuring and pivoting the business.

Data Flywheel Assessment

⚪ COSMETIC — AI Infrastructure, Not AI Software
1
Does GEV have proprietary training data no competitor can access?
Partially — GEV has extensive operational data from 7,000 turbines and 57,000 wind turbines (digital twins, performance monitoring). This data improves the efficiency of their own servicing. However, it does not create a user-attraction loop — customers don't come to GEV because the AI is better; they come because only GEV can service their GEV turbines.
2
Does more data measurably improve the AI or service?
Yes — GEV's Predix digital twin platform uses turbine operational data to predict maintenance needs and optimize efficiency. More turbines in service = better predictive models. BUT: this is a one-directional improvement (GEV's servicing gets better) — not a self-reinforcing loop that attracts more customers to GEV specifically.
3
Does better AI attract more users, completing the flywheel loop?
No. Customers choose GEV turbines because of turbine performance, pricing, and delivery — not because GEV has better AI servicing. The switching cost is the installed hardware, not the software intelligence. GEV is fundamentally a Level 1 infrastructure beneficiary — it wins because AI needs electricity, not because its AI improves.

Flywheel Score: 3/10 — AI feature, not moat. GEV benefits from AI as a demand driver for its core products, which is an enormous and real advantage. But the flywheel logic doesn't apply to Level 1 infrastructure. This is the right classification — not a weakness, just a different type of AI opportunity.

Key Risks

Bear Case Risks

1. AI capex pause — If a major technology cost shock (geopolitical, energy, model efficiency breakthrough) causes hyperscalers to pause data center buildout, GEV's order book growth stalls. Backlog gives 3–4 years of revenue buffer but growth premium evaporates.

2. Wind segment liabilities — Vineyard Wind lawsuit + ongoing offshore wind losses could crystallize into material charges. The segment is actively restructuring but remains a wildcard.

3. Execution risk at scale — Doubling EBITDA margins from 2026 to 2028 requires flawless supply chain execution, turbine delivery ramp, and Prolec integration. Any quality/delivery failure at a key data center customer would be damaging.

4. Rate environment — At 70x+ P/E and 2.3% FCF yield, GEV is acutely sensitive to Treasury yields. If 10-yr yields rise to 5%+, the negative ERP widens further and P/E multiple compression could be severe.

Bull Case Reinforcements

1. Sold-out through 2030 — CEO Strazik expects gas turbine slots to be sold through 2030 by year-end 2026. This provides near-complete revenue visibility through the decade — extraordinarily unusual for an industrial company.
