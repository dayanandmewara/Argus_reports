Overview

Business model, category classification rationale, and framework adaptations applied

Framework adaptation notice (read first): Aadhar Housing Finance is a housing finance company (HFC) — a leveraged lending balance sheet, not an operating business with conventional FCF. BQV v5.0's default machinery (ROIC-as-moat-proof, FCF-based Owner Earnings, DCF, O'Glove CFO/NI test) is built for non-financial compounders and is structurally distorted when applied literally to a lender: operating cash flow is mechanically negative every year the loan book grows (disbursements score as operating outflows; the funding for them sits in financing activities), so a naive DCF or CFO/NI≥80% test would flag a healthy, fast-growing HFC as "failing" for reasons that have nothing to do with earnings quality. Three adaptations are applied throughout this report, each flagged again where used: (1) ROE/ROA substitute for ROIC as the moat-proof metric, (2) a 2-stage Residual Income / Justified P/B model substitutes for DCF in Tab 5 (this is the standard practitioner equivalent for banks/NBFCs — same discounting logic, balance-sheet-native inputs), (3) O'Glove Tests 1–3 are re-mapped to lender-appropriate analogs (ALM/LCR, GNPA/NNPA trend, spread consistency) rather than applied literally. The sector-growth table in Tab 1 also has no native "Affordable HFC" row — the closest is "NBFCs / Microfinance" — so a bespoke Stress/Base/Accel range is derived directly from housing-finance-specific market data rather than borrowed unmodified from the generic NBFC row.

What the company does

Aadhar is a housing finance company focused almost exclusively on the low-income segment — average ticket size ~₹10 lakh, average loan-to-value ~60%, loans capped below ₹15 lakh. V The customer base is disproportionately informal-income: self-employed traders, daily-wage workers, first-generation formal borrowers with no ITR or payslip. Aadhar's underwriting is built around assessing actual cash flow rather than requiring formal income documentation — the operating philosophy that differentiates HFCs like Aadhar from banks, which find sub-₹15L tickets administratively uneconomical to originate and service. E

FY26 (year to March 2026): AUM ₹30,571 Cr (+20% YoY), 626 branches across 22 states/UTs and 550+ districts, 336,000+ live loan accounts, PAT ₹1,108 Cr (+22% YoY). V The company was originally incorporated in 1990 as Vysya Bank Housing Finance, restructured through DHFL Vysya Housing Finance, merged with a separately-incorporated Aadhar Housing Finance Pvt Ltd in 2017, IPO'd on NSE/BSE in May 2024 raising ~₹3,000 Cr, and has been Blackstone-sponsored throughout. Management

Capital allocation, promoter structure, and the February 2026 change-of-control — read carefully, not mechanically

Why this isn't a mechanical pledge-gate trigger. BQV v5.0's promoter-pledge rule (>40% pledged = automatic AVOID) exists to catch margin-call risk — a promoter who has borrowed against shares and can be forced to sell into a falling market. What happened here in April 2026 is a different instrument: National Housing Bank required the new controlling shareholder to sign a Non-Disposal Undertaking (NDU) on 65.19% of its stake V — a regulator-mandated lock-up preventing the new promoter from flipping control immediately after acquisition, standard practice for HFCs NHB refinances. It restricts sale; it does not collateralise a loan, and there is no margin-call mechanism attached. Applying the pledge-severity gate literally here would mischaracterise a governance safeguard as a leverage red flag. It is still treated below as a genuine, monitor-worthy governance event — just not an automatic AVOID.

But it is still a real governance event: a new legal promoter entity, a new post-transaction cap table, and a regulator-imposed lock-up that didn't exist before. The market's own read reflects this — Marketsmojo's automated peer-comparison tool currently scores Aadhar's management risk as "Average," versus "Good" or "Excellent" for several direct peers (Aavas, Home First, India Shelter) V, and sell-side commentary explicitly flagged "governance concerns" around the transition V.

Valuation

P/B Gordon Growth (2-stage Residual Income) substitutes for DCF — computed, not eyeballed, in Python for this report

Why DCF is skipped entirely. Owner Earnings and free cash flow are not meaningful for a lender whose operating cash flow is mechanically negative while the loan book grows. The rigorous practitioner substitute — used by Damodaran and standard bank-equity research desks alike — is a 2-stage Residual Income / Justified P/B model: book value compounds at ROE×retention through an explicit stage, then a Gordon Growth multiple is applied to terminal ROE, terminal growth, and cost of equity. Four independent methods are run below; they do not agree with each other, and that disagreement is itself the finding.
