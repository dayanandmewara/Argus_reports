Business Overview & Why This Report

Micron Technology designs, manufactures, and sells DRAM memory and NAND flash storage to data centers, cloud hyperscalers, mobile OEMs, automotive, and embedded markets. Founded in 1978 and headquartered in Boise, Idaho, Micron is the only American company manufacturing High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) at scale — the memory technology that sits physically on every Nvidia GPU powering AI training and inference.

The stock was analysed under the Category B (High-Growth Global Compounder) framework because: revenue CAGR (FY2024→FY2026E) exceeds 125%, the AI memory market is still underpenetrated relative to compute demand, ROIC is on an extraordinary J-curve, and FCF is accelerating asymmetrically. The 🟢 AI WIDENER overlay is the strongest possible — Micron's product IS the AI infrastructure layer.

Framework adaptation: Unlike traditional Cat B, Micron carries a meaningful cyclical risk dimension requiring scenario analysis. However, the 16 SCAs ($100B minimum), the HBM supply shortage persisting "beyond 2027" per management, and the structural transformation of memory from commodity to strategic AI asset justify treating this as a structural rather than purely cyclical position.

Moat Assessment — Five Sources
1. HBM Oligopoly — Efficient Scale + Cost Advantage
Only 3 companies on earth can make HBM at scale: SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron. Building a fab costs $15-25B and takes 4-5 years from concept to full production. The semiconductor process knowledge required is measured in decades. Chinese manufacturers (CXMT etc.) face technology gaps of several generations and export control restrictions on critical equipment. This oligopoly is structurally durable. [VERIFIED — industry data]
★★★★★
EXCEPTIONAL
Stress test: STRENGTHENS in stress — if AI capex slows, demand falls BUT supply also cannot be quickly added (5yr lead time for new capacity), keeping Micron's HBM position intact.
2. Technology Qualification Switching Costs
Once Nvidia qualifies Micron's HBM for a specific GPU architecture (e.g. Blackwell Next, Vera Rubin, Vera Rubin Ultra), switching to a competitor requires 12-24 months of re-qualification engineering. This creates per-generation stickiness. Micron has qualified HBM3E on major AI accelerators and is ramping HBM4 at roughly 2x the speed of HBM3E — building a head start for next-generation AI chips. [VERIFIED — Earnings Call]
★★★★★

3. Manufacturing Process Moat + 1-Gamma Node
Micron's 1-gamma DRAM node (among the most advanced manufactured in the US) delivers better performance and lower power than prior generations. The G9 NAND node is ramping fastest in company history. These process transitions represent decades of cumulative engineering knowledge that cannot be replicated quickly — even by well-funded competitors. [VERIFIED — Earnings Call]
★★★★

4. US Government Strategic Asset + CHIPS Act
Micron is the ONLY American HBM manufacturer. In a geopolitical environment where semiconductor supply security is a top policy priority, this distinction creates a structural advantage: preferential access to CHIPS Act subsidies, Made-in-America mandates for government AI procurement, and a degree of protection from export-control dynamics that disadvantage Asian competitors in certain US markets. Goldman Sachs Bear Case — The ~$400 Target Unpacked
BEAR CASE ANALYSIS — Goldman Sachs ~$400 target

Goldman's thesis: Memory remains cyclical. SK Hynix M15X (mid-2027), Samsung P5 (2028), and Micron's new Idaho/Taiwan/NY fabs could all come online around the same window, creating synchronized oversupply similar to 2023. At a cycle trough, Micron could earn a fraction of peak EPS and trade at a low multiple, implying a price well below current levels.

The counter-case (4 specific points):

1. SCA contracts guarantee floor revenue. Even if spot HBM prices fall meaningfully, the SCA floor prices protect roughly 40% of revenue from declining at all. Goldman's framework implicitly assumes a 2023-style revenue collapse — SCAs are designed specifically to prevent that.

2. HBM demand is not a single-product cycle. It compounds across generations — HBM3E → HBM4 → HBM4E → HBM5 — each requiring new qualifications and delivering more revenue per GPU. Agentic AI requires materially more memory than simple inference, so the demand curve may be steeper than a pure cyclical framework assumes.

3. New fab supply risk may be overstated in timing. All three manufacturers' new fabs require 12-18 months of qualification ramp after construction completes. Even if all fabs open in 2027, usable HBM output at meaningful scale likely doesn't appear until 2028-2029.

4. A ~$400 target implies a low single-digit P/E on a depressed EPS base — i.e. it requires both a 2023-style downcycle in earnings AND a distressed multiple simultaneously. The SCA floor makes the first leg of that scenario (EPS collapsing to near-zero) considerably less likely than in prior cycles, though it does not eliminate cyclical risk entirely — investors should treat Goldman's view as a legitimate tail-risk scenario worth monitoring, not a fringe call.

B — HBM as AI Infrastructure: Why This Is a Compelling AI Trade
The Physical Reality of AI: Memory Is a Real Bottleneck

Every large language model — from major labs across the industry — runs on GPU clusters, and every modern Nvidia AI GPU requires multiple HBM stacks, each containing tens of gigabytes of memory, for well over 100GB of HBM per GPU. A single large hyperscaler cluster with tens of thousands of GPUs requires hundreds of thousands of HBM stacks. At roughly $300-500 per stack, this represents tens of billions of dollars in HBM cost per major data center build-out.

Without HBM, AI accelerators cannot move data fast enough to run large models at scale. HBM is not optional — it is physically integrated into the GPU package itself. This is part of why HBM demand has tracked AI capex spending so closely.

As AI usage shifts from simple inference toward training-intensive workloads, agentic AI (continuous, memory-hungry), and eventually machine-to-machine AI, memory requirements per unit of compute are widely expected to increase rather than decrease. This creates a demand-escalation mechanism that doesn't depend solely on more AI deployments — it can also come from each existing deployment needing more memory over time.
