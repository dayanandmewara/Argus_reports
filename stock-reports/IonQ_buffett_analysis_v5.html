What IonQ Does

Trapped-ion quantum computing: IonQ builds quantum computers using individual ytterbium atoms — ionized and suspended in electromagnetic traps — as qubits (quantum bits). This "trapped-ion" approach offers fundamental advantages over competitors' superconducting circuits (Google, IBM): all-to-all qubit connectivity (any qubit directly interacts with any other), longer coherence times, and higher gate fidelities. IonQ holds the world record for two-qubit gate fidelity at 99.99% — a number that matters enormously because quantum error correction overhead scales inversely with gate quality.

Revenue model: (1) Quantum Computing as a Service (QCaaS) — cloud access via AWS Braket, Microsoft Azure Quantum, Google Cloud Marketplace, and IonQ's own cloud. Pay-per-use or subscription. (2) Hardware System Sales — selling physical quantum computers (KISTI South Korea got a 100-qubit 5th-generation system). (3) Government contracts — R&D, design, construction of specialised quantum hardware. (4) Quantum networking and sensing (nascent).

The SkyWater acquisition thesis: By internalising SkyWater's fabrication capabilities, IonQ is betting that hardware independence will be the deciding factor in the race toward fault-tolerant quantum computing, potentially cutting design-to-production cycles. SkyWater is the only US-based pure-play semiconductor foundry — critical for government contracts requiring domestic supply chains and for IonQ's Electronic Qubit Control (EQC) roadmap, replacing bulky expensive lasers with integrated electronic signals generated on-chip. If EQC works at volume, it enables scaling from hundreds to hundreds of thousands of qubits.

The sector status as of March 2026: Quantum computing is genuinely Phase 1→2. The UK announced a £2 billion initiative to roll out quantum computers at scale by the early 2030s. Germany, Korea (KISTI), and Cambridge have committed real institutional money. But the technology has NOT yet demonstrated "quantum advantage" on commercially relevant workloads. Current quantum computers solve toy problems at quantum speed and real problems at classical speed. Fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) — the threshold for genuine advantage — requires thousands of logical qubits, which requires millions of physical qubits, which has not been achieved by anyone.

Layer 3 — The Quantum Adoption Phase Problem

Quantum computing is Phase 1 of adoption — and NISQ era limitations are the binding constraint
The quantum field operates in the NISQ era (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum). NISQ machines (like IonQ's current systems) have insufficient qubits and fidelity for fault-tolerant computation. They can solve certain optimization and simulation problems faster than classical computers for small instances, but classical computers still win on most commercially relevant workloads. The transition from NISQ to Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing (FTQC) requires:
→ 1,000+ logical qubits → Requires millions of physical qubits → Requires error correction overhead of ~1,000 physical per logical qubit
→ IonQ's current 6th-generation system: ~100 algorithmic qubits (not the same as logical fault-tolerant qubits)
→ IonQ's 2029 roadmap target: 2 million physical qubits → The gap between 100 algorithmic and 2M physical is enormous

This is not a critique of IonQ specifically — it applies to Google, IBM, and every quantum company. The Phase 1 constraint is physics, not execution.

Section B — Quantum-AI Relationship (Not a Flywheel)

Why Quantum is NOT AI (for this framework):

AI uses classical computers (CPUs, GPUs) running gradient descent, matrix multiplication, and statistical inference. The AI "intelligence" emerges from training on data. Quantum computing uses quantum mechanical properties to compute in fundamentally different ways — solving specific problem types (optimization, simulation, factoring) exponentially faster than classical computers. The two technologies are distinct and complementary.

Where they intersect (the adjacency opportunity):

1. Quantum ML: Quantum algorithms could accelerate certain ML workloads (variational circuits, quantum kernel methods). Still theoretical for most practical cases.
2. Quantum simulation for AI hardware design: Drug discovery, materials science simulation, financial optimization — all of which could feed back into better classical AI systems.
3. Post-Quantum Cryptography: As quantum computers improve, classical encryption (RSA, elliptic curve) becomes vulnerable. PQC is a regulatory compliance market driven by quantum advancement — IonQ Quantum Security division addresses this.

Framework implication: IonQ's AI NEUTRAL status means the AI Alpha rows (6a, 6b, 6c) in the growth analysis contribute ZERO. The quantum sector's growth must stand on its own merits — it cannot be boosted by the AI adoption wave that is fuelling companies like Planet, NVIDIA, Netweb, and ASML.
