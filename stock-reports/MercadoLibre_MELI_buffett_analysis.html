The Mispricing Thesis — Why -38% Is an Opportunity, Not a Warning

MELI's stock has fallen from $2,645 to $1,641 — a 38% decline — on the back of a single quarter where EPS missed by $0.63 per share. The reasons for the miss: (1) lower free shipping threshold in Brazil increased volume but compressed margins; (2) aggressive credit card issuance ($3M cards in Q4) elevated credit provisions; (3) heavy 1P commerce and cross-border trade investment. Every one of these is a deliberate growth investment, not a sign of competitive deterioration.

Consider what was happening simultaneously: Revenue beat by $310M. Items sold accelerated to +43% (from +39% in Q4 2024). Unique buyers crossed 80M for the first time. Advertising grew 67%. AUM grew 78%. Credit portfolio nearly doubled. Fintech MAU maintained 30% growth for 10 consecutive quarters. Brazil's Mercado Pago reached #1 NPS. This is not a business that is struggling. This is a business that is deliberately sacrificing short-term EPS for long-term ecosystem dominance — the Amazon playbook, executed in markets that are still 20-30 years behind the US in e-commerce and financial inclusion.
