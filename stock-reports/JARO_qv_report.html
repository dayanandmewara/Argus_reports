Company Overview
Business Model

Jaro Institute of Technology Management and Research Limited (NSE: JARO) is India's oldest and only consistently profitable, self-funded EdTech company. Founded in 2009 by first-generation entrepreneur Dr. Sanjay Namdeo Salunkhe, the company acts as a program management services intermediary — it does not create academic content or award degrees directly, but facilitates student enrollments and provides digital/physical infrastructure for partner universities to deliver online and hybrid programs.


The company operates through two modes: (1) Direct Collection — Jaro collects fees from students and remits to universities; (2) Referral — fees paid directly to universities who remit a commission to Jaro. Revenue is recognized over the course duration, creating a deferred revenue model that front-loads cash costs against deferred income recognition.

Why This Stock Is In The Series

JARO is a rare listed pure-play on India's booming online higher-education market — a sector projected to grow at 20–25% CAGR through 2030. The company has delivered exceptional fundamentals (ROE ~36%, EBITDA margin ~33%) but faces a critical structural concern: negative operating cash flow driven by marketing front-loading and rising working-capital days.


The stock listed at ₹890 in September 2025 and has since corrected ~50% — presenting a potential entry opportunity if cash-flow concerns prove temporary. This is a classic Category B high-growth quality-vs-price tension that the QV framework is designed to adjudicate.

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get. But you must be sure the value is real — not just reported." — Warren Buffett (paraphrased)

JARO Education is a genuinely interesting Category B watch candidate — not yet a conviction buy.

The bull case is clear: India's only listed, profitable, self-funded EdTech company with IIT/IIM exclusive programs, 17 years of continuous profitability, excellent ROE/ROCE metrics (35–37%), strong gross margin expansion, and a sector growing at 20–25% CAGR. The stock has corrected 50% from IPO price, taking it to levels where the base intrinsic value exceeds the current price by ~35%.

The bear case is equally clear: Negative operating cash flow in both FY24 and FY25, rising debtor days (52 days), working capital expanding to 227 days, IPO proceeds sitting undeployed, H1 FY26 revenue growth of only +4.6% YoY (a dramatic deceleration), and the promoter selling ₹280 Cr at IPO at what proved to be a peak price.

The framework verdict: Four pillars are solid-to-good (B to B+), but earnings quality is SUSPECT per O'Glove tests, and the convergence of pillars is insufficient for a BUY at current price given the conditional nature of the value. The stock needs OCF to turn positive before the financial quality concern resolves. Until then, WATCH with disciplined upgrade conditions.
