Business Overview
What NVIDIA Does

NVIDIA Corporation designs accelerated computing platforms — the silicon, software, and system architectures that power artificial intelligence. Founded in 1993 and headquartered in Santa Clara, California, NVIDIA operates a fabless model: it designs chips but outsources manufacturing to TSMC and Samsung, giving it exceptional capital-lightness relative to its revenue scale.

The company operates in two reportable segments: Compute & Networking (data centre AI accelerators, networking, automotive AI) — which generated $193.7B of FY2026's $215.9B total revenue — and Graphics (GeForce gaming GPUs, professional visualisation, Omniverse).

NVIDIA's core product advantage is not the GPU itself but the CUDA software ecosystem built on top of it — 20 years of developer tools, libraries (cuDNN, TensorRT, NCCL, RAPIDS), and an installed base of millions of researchers who have optimised every AI model on NVIDIA hardware. Competitors must match both the silicon AND the software. This is the defining moat of the AI era.

Why This Stock Is In the Series

NVIDIA is the keystone infrastructure company of the AI supercycle. Without NVIDIA GPUs, the foundational models that power OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and every major cloud AI service cannot be trained. This is an infrastructure monopoly in its prime expansion phase.

The CUDA data flywheel is confirmed: every AI researcher who writes custom CUDA kernels deepens the switching cost. Every NVIDIA GPU sold into a lab deepens the developer training base. Every model trained on CUDA optimises for NVIDIA hardware. The loop compounds.

However, three structural risks demand rigorous analysis: (1) hyperscaler-designed custom ASICs (Google TPU, AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia, Meta MTIA) that could erode 15–25% of addressable revenue over 5–7 years; (2) the China export control ceiling removing a formerly meaningful revenue segment; (3) the sheer scale of current earnings means the absolute growth required to justify $4.5T gets harder each year.

This is a generational asset — but requires a margin of safety even for generational assets.

Moat Sources
CUDA Software Ecosystem ★★★★★
20-year head start. 4M+ developers trained on CUDA. Thousands of optimised libraries: cuDNN, TensorRT, NCCL, RAPIDS, cuBLAS. Every major AI framework (PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX, Hugging Face) is CUDA-native. Switching to ROCm or any alternative requires months of rewriting custom kernels, performance regression, and developer retraining. Closed-source and legally protected. This is the defining moat of the AI era.
Hardware-Software Co-Design Flywheel ★★★★★
NVIDIA designs Tensor Cores specifically to accelerate CUDA's linear algebra primitives. CUDA libraries are hand-tuned for each new GPU architecture. This co-design creates a mutually reinforcing lock-in: the hardware performs best with CUDA, and CUDA performs best on NVIDIA hardware. Competitors must match both simultaneously — a near-impossible catch-up challenge. Blackwell Tensor Cores optimised for FP4/FP8 precision in cuDNN → performance advantage compounds each generation.
NVLink / Networking Switching Cost ★★★★
GB200 NVL72 rack-scale systems use NVLink to interconnect 72 GPUs at 1.8 TB/s bandwidth — 7× InfiniBand alternatives. Once a hyperscaler deploys NVL72, switching to a non-NVIDIA system requires full rack replacement, network redesign, and software re-optimisation. Estimated switching cost per 1,000-GPU cluster: $50–100M+. Quantum InfiniBand (for training clusters) + Spectrum Ethernet (inference) create a full networking layer dependency.
Talent Magnetism & Research Credibility ★★★★
The best AI researchers want to work with the best tools. CUDA's ubiquity means NVIDIA hardware is where every major AI paper is developed and benchmarked. This creates a prestige network effect — researchers demand NVIDIA GPUs, which pressures labs and companies to buy NVIDIA, which generates more research on NVIDIA, reinforcing the ecosystem. Jensen Huang's technical credibility (appeared at CVPR, NeurIPS) further cements this.
Hyperscaler Dependency (Double-Edged) ★★★
AWS, Microsoft, Google, and Meta collectively represent est. 50%+ of NVIDIA's Data Centre revenue. This concentration is a moat today (captive demand, price-setting power) but a risk tomorrow (hyperscalers are each building custom ASICs). Google's TPU, AWS Trainium 2, Microsoft Maia, Meta MTIA are all progressing. The hyperscaler ASIC threat is real — but each custom chip serves only one hyperscaler's proprietary models, while CUDA serves every model on every customer.

Management Concerns & Red Flags

1. Aggressive Buyback at Peak Valuations: Repurchasing $33.7B of stock at 40–50× P/E is debatable capital allocation. If NVIDIA's stock corrects 30–40% in a macro downturn, these buybacks destroy capital. Jensen's large personal stake (aligned) mitigates but doesn't eliminate this concern.

2. China Export Control Navigation: The $4.5B H20 inventory charge suggests management was slow to anticipate the final H20 export restrictions. Communications around China have been reactive rather than proactive. The "Q1 FY2027 guidance explicitly excludes China DC compute" disclosure should have been made earlier.

3. Customer Concentration Risk: ~50%+ of DC revenue from 5 hyperscaler customers. These customers have enormous leverage and are actively building alternatives. Management has not publicly disclosed detailed customer concentration — this is a material risk that could be disclosed more transparently.

4. Supply Chain Concentration: Manufacturing entirely dependent on TSMC's Taiwan facilities. Geopolitical risk is existential — a Taiwan conflict or TSMC capacity disruption would halt NVIDIA's entire production. NVIDIA has no fab redundancy. Jensen has acknowledged this as a risk without a near-term mitigation plan.
